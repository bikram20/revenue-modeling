üß† EXPLANATION: Why Does the Exit Run-Rate Method Overcommit?

The Double-Counting Problem

Key Insight: Your December 2025 exit MRR already includes all the expansion, upsells, and growth that happened during 2025!

‚ùå What the Exit Run-Rate Method Does Wrong:

  1. Takes December exit MRR - which already includes 2025's expansions
  2. Multiplies by 12 - assumes this grown rate continues all year
  3. Applies NDR on top - adds expansion AGAIN to already-expanded revenue
Result: You're counting the same customers' expansion twice - once in the exit MRR, and again when applying NDR.

‚úÖ What the Cohort-Based NDR Method Does Correctly:

  1. Identifies the base cohort - customers you had on Jan 1, 2025
  2. Applies NDR only to that base - this is their natural expansion in 2026
  3. Separately accounts for new customers - growth from acquisition during 2026
Result: Each customer cohort's expansion is counted exactly once, at the right time.

üìä Think of it this way:

If a customer starts 2025 at $100K ARR and expands to $110K by December:

  • Method 1 says: "They're at $110K now, so in 2026 they'll be $110K √ó 1.10 = $121K"
  • Method 2 says: "They started at $100K, so in 2026 they'll be $100K √ó 1.10 = $110K"

Method 1 gave them 21% growth instead of 10%! That's the double-count.

üí° Pro Tip: NDR should always be applied to a point-in-time cohort (like Jan 1 ARR), not to a run-rate that already includes a year's worth of growth.
